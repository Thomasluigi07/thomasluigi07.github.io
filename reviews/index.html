YO-KAI WATCH 3 (3DS)

LEVEL-5

YO-KAI WATCH 3 is currently the last game in the mainline series outside of Asia. It's a very long game, with a LOT of content. Even when you finish it, there's still a lot more stuff you can do! This game also switches up the battle system from the previous games. Instead of having a wheel you have to spin around to change where your Yo-Kai are on the battle field, it's now grid-based. You move the Yo-Kai medals to where you want them to be, and bam! After 1 "turn", they move there. You can also dodge Yo-Kai soultimates using the grid. In previous games, it would simply be an unavoidable attack if you didn't cancel it. The game's dialoge is pretty funny, and at the start of the game I love the moment where everyone is speaking in some yankee accent and the main character (Nate) is hella confused on what they're saying. I liked this game a LOT. I put around 35 hours into this game, which I think is the most I've ever put into for an RPG game.

My rating: 9/10

Klonoa 2: Lunatea's Veil (PS2)

NAMCO

Klonoa 2: Lunatea's Veil is so fucking good. Literally. I'm not joking. I can't believe I put off playing this game until I got a copy of the PS2 version, and oh my god, this game is a masterpiece. Klonoa 2's controls and gameplay is close to the first game, dare I say 1:1. This doesn't mean it's just the same stuff again, but "2", Klonoa 2 adds in new elements and stage gimmicks that I find so awesome, such as the launcher cannon things and the bosses. The levels that stood out the most for me were "Maze of Memories" and "Hyuponia". The game's soundtrack is just so damn good I listen to it in my free time. I also like how Klonoa seems to have learned from his past. I would recommend playing the PS2 version if you can first because it's a lot less jank than the remake, however if you don't mind it, or you need easy mode and speeding up cutscenes, play the remake instead. Overall, I think Klonoa 2 is a fantastic sequel to Klonoa 1, and I can't wait for more. It's a shame Klonoa is still lesser-known, as literally every mainline game that has come out of the Klonoa franchise is extremely good.

My rating: 10/10

マリオアーティスト ペイントスタジオ (64DD)

Mario Artist: Paint Studio (64DD)

Software Creations

Mario Artist: Paint Studio is the sequel to SNES's Mario Paint. What's new in this tool however is well... too much to mention here! With the 64DD diskette's save size, you can save lots, and I mean lots, of drawings! You can also make animations now, and there's a lot of stamps you can use to add to your picture too. I love drawing something nice and then ruining it with some stamps of Mario & others. There's also a 3D world where you can explore around, take pictures of things that you can draw on later and also edit the texture of some objects (I turned a dolphin's texture into Mario). There's also multiplayer, but it's heavily limited in what you can do, however you can grief your friend's drawing at any time by clicking the Rocket. However, this game is not that easy to draw with without an N64 mouse, so using one is basically recommended if you want to have more fine control. Oh, and I forgot to mention: you can use pictures taken with the Game Boy Camera and the Capture Pak. However, there's no Gnat Attack, unfortunately. Mario Artist: Paint Studio is a really good game, but I wish there was more you could do.

My rating: 8/10

マリオアーティスト タレントスタジオ (64DD)

Mario Artist: Talent Studio (64DD)

Nintendo

Mario Artist: Talent Studio is one of the most fun character creator tool I've ever used before. In this tool, you can make your own character (called "Talents"), and you can watch them dance in "Show Time" or make your own movies. The talent customisation is very intricate, with the ability to even use a picture you have taken with the Game Boy Camera, Capture Pak or any drawings you made in Mario Artist: Paint Studio as a face, texture for clothing, and more! You can also give your character a voice using the Capture Pak as well. The movie editor may take a while to learn but is fun to master, and the sample movies included are very unique and bizzare. You can even import 3D models made in Mario Artist: Polygon Studio as objects in your movie too! There's also a gallery where you can show off your movies and talents. The music for this game is really, really good too. Mario Artist: Talent Studio is an amazing tool and hopefully it (along with the other 64DD titles) gets re-released some day on Nintendo Classics, especially with the Nintendo Switch 2 having a mouse mode..

My rating: 10/10

マリオアーティスト ポリゴンスタジオ (64DD)

Mario Artist: Polygon Studio (64DD)

Nichimen Graphics

Mario Artist: Polygon Studio is the last game in the Mario Artist series (not counting Mario Artist: Communication Kit). I was suprised at how simple to use the model creator is, and I actually preferred using it over any other 3D modeling tool I've ever used. The model creator lets you use simple shapes such as a cube as a base (Blender users HATE the CUBE!!) and some other objects to start with if you're not feeling like making a model such as Mario himself or the N64 controller. Once you've inserted a base you can pretty much manipulate every point of the model and go wild, add more vertecies and faces (until you hit the polygon limit). You can paint onto your model with ease, with some pre-made stamps; However, if you decide to edit the model after painting you lose all of the paint, most likely due to hardware limitations. That's not all however. There's an entirely seperate creator where you can add machine parts to your model such as legs, a mouth that speaks, wheels, and many more you can unlock in the semi-open world called the "Experimental World" by going up to toasters and speaking to sentient toast... yeah. There's a lot you can do in this tool, and as mentioned earlier you can put the Polygon Studio diskette into Talent Studio and use your models in the movie editor. You can also make a set with all your model creations, take a picture and use it in Paint Studio to draw over. The interconnectivity of all these tools is really cool. Finally, there's two minigames, one called Boom Beat and the other called Go-Go Park. Boom Beat is a precursor to WarioWare Inc and is very challenging to get a high score on, while Go-Go Park is a weird minigame where you have to wind up your creation and send it flying to reach the "safe" zone, otherwise it will EXPLODE to smithereens if you don't reach it or fly off the edge if you charged up too much. Overall, Mario Artist: Polygon Studio was a tool I had a lot of fun with.

My rating: 9/10

エフゼロ エックス エクスパンション キット (64DD)

F-Zero X Expansion Kit (64DD)

Nintendo

F-Zero X Expansion Kit is pretty much DLC before DLC was really feasable. There's 12 new tracks, it adds an in-depth track editor to the game (apparently it is pretty much the same the developers used to make the tracks in the game). I had a lot of fun making really dumb tracks that you can die easily on. There's a car editor but I didn't really use it as I stick to just the default. It also adds stereo music and some new music tracks, such as a really good remix of Mario Kart 64's Rainbow Road. You can now save time trial ghosts onto the diskette instead of a Controller Pak, which is a really nice feature because my (bootleg) Controller Pak isn't working anymore. One extra thing you can also do is use the Expansion Kit diskette with Mario Artist: Paint studio, and you'll get some images of F-Zero X renders you can use in your artwork. Overall I had a lot of fun with this and adds on to F-Zero X in a good way.

My rating: 8/10
